================
  Project Notes
================
## Dataset Selection

Raw Sanger sequencing data were obtained from a publicly available Zenodo dataset containing fungal and bacterial barcode records associated with *Cyrtomium fortunei* leaf isolates.

From the available seven samples, Sample 1 was selected for workflow demonstration because it contained:

* forward and reverse `.ab1` chromatogram files
* exported sequence files
* associated metadata

The goal of the project was not species discovery, but understanding the complete Sanger bioinformatics workflow from raw chromatogram data to phylogenetic analysis.

---

# Initial Chromatogram Inspection

The forward and reverse '.ab1' files were opened in Chromas for manual quality inspection.

Observations:

* The beginning regions of both reads showed broad peaks, overlapping signals, and lower confidence base calling.
* The middle portions showed clean and sharp peaks with good separation.
* Signal quality deteriorated toward the end of reads.
* Some terminal regions contained no usable signal.

Approximate usable regions:

* Forward read: positions ~50–250
* Reverse read: positions ~70–270

This demonstrated the typical Sanger sequencing quality pattern:
poor-quality start → high-quality middle → degraded end.

---

# Alignment Troubleshooting

The forward and reverse reads were exported as FASTA files and loaded into MEGA12.

Initial alignment showed:

* mismatches at the beginning and end
* several gaps
* poor overlap in low-quality regions

The middle region aligned correctly.

At first, the reverse read did not align properly because reverse complement conversion had not yet been considered.

After reverse-complementing the reverse read, the overlap improved significantly.

This helped in understanding:

* sequence orientation
* forward/reverse sequencing logic
* importance of reverse complement conversion

---

# Manual Trimming

Instead of using automated trimming tools, low-quality terminal regions were manually removed using a text editor.

Problematic regions were identified by:

* mismatches in alignment
* noisy chromatogram peaks
* poor overlap

After trimming:

* the alignment quality improved substantially
* forward and reverse reads showed nearly complete overlap
* gaps and mismatches were reduced

This demonstrated how sequence quality directly affects alignment accuracy.

---

# Consensus Sequence Generation

Since the trimmed reads showed complete agreement in the overlap region, one cleaned sequence was selected as the consensus sequence for downstream analysis.

This step helped in understanding:

* consensus sequence generation
* overlap validation
* sequence confidence assessment

---

# BLAST Analysis

The consensus FASTA sequence was analyzed using NCBI BLAST.

Observations:

* Multiple top hits showed 100% identity and 100% query coverage.
* Most matches corresponded to Annulohypoxylon-associated ITS sequences.

This demonstrated:

* sequence similarity searching
* interpretation of BLAST metrics
* importance of query coverage and identity values

Learned that:
BLAST similarity alone does not guarantee definitive species identification.

---

# Multiple Sequence Alignment

A total of 17 reference sequences from top BLAST hits were downloaded and aligned together with the sample sequence using MUSCLE in MEGA12.

During this stage:

* MUSCLE and ClustalW differences were explored
* codon vs non-codon alignment concepts were learned
* importance of homologous alignment positions became clearer

Since ITS is a non-coding region, regular MUSCLE alignment was used instead of MUSCLE (Codon).

---

# Phylogenetic Tree Construction

A Neighbor-Joining tree with bootstrap analysis was constructed in MEGA12.

Settings used:

* p-distance model
* pairwise deletion
* 100 bootstrap replicates

Initial bootstrap support for the sample branch was approximately 61.
After reconstructing the tree, bootstrap support changed to approximately 77.

This helped in understanding:

* bootstrap variability
* stochastic nature of phylogenetic inference
* phylogenetic uncertainty
* difference between topology and visual proximity

An important realization during interpretation:
the closest evolutionary relationship is determined by shared nodes (topology), not by visual distance between labels on the tree.

The sample sequence clustered within an Annulohypoxylon-related clade and showed closest affinity to ON557328.1.

---

# Major Learning Outcomes

This project provided hands-on understanding of:

* chromatogram interpretation
* sequence quality assessment
* trimming logic
* reverse complement handling
* sequence alignment
* BLAST analysis
* phylogenetic tree construction
* bootstrap interpretation
* evolutionary relationship analysis

The project demonstrated how raw sequencing signals are transformed into biologically interpretable results through a stepwise bioinformatics workflow.
